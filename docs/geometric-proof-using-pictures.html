3.1 Geometric proof using pictures

The FrischâWaughâLovell theorem is perhaps best explained using pictures. The following is taken from Davidson and McKinnon (1993) monograph We consider for simplicity the bivariate case and two non-parallel vectors \(\mathbf{X}_1\) and \(\mathbf{X}_2\). The yellow line illustrate the usual orthogonal decomposition \({\boldsymbol{y}} = {\mathbf{H}}_{{\mathbf{X}}}{{\boldsymbol{y}}}+ {\mathbf{M}}_{{\mathbf{X}}}{{\boldsymbol{y}}}\), where \({\mathbf{H}}_{{\mathbf{X}}}{{\boldsymbol{y}}}\) is the segment \(OB\) and \({\mathbf{M}}_{{\mathbf{X}}}{{\boldsymbol{y}}}\) is the segment \(BA\). A similar decomposition can be performed in terms of the sole \({\mathbf{X}}_1\), namely through \({\mathbf{H}}_{{\mathbf{X}}_1}{{\boldsymbol{y}}}\) corresponding to the line segment \(OC\) and \({\mathbf{M}}_{{\mathbf{X}}_1}{{\boldsymbol{y}}}\) corresponding to \(CA\).

Figure 3.1: Orthogonal decomposition into residuals and fitted values

The right-angled triangle \(ABC\) is orthogonal to \({\mathbf{X}}_1\). We deduce that the segment \(CB\) is \({\mathbf{M}}_{{\mathbf{X}}_1}{\mathbf{H}}_{{\mathbf{X}}}{{\boldsymbol{y}}}\).

Figure 3.2: Projection onto spane spanned by the regressors

We may also look at the plane \({\mathsf{span}}({\mathbf{X}})\) of the vector \({{\boldsymbol{y}}}\) and its fitted value. More specifically, consider the decomposition \({\mathbf{H}}_{{\mathbf{X}}}{{\boldsymbol{y}}}= {\mathbf{X}}_1{\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}}}_1 + {\mathbf{X}}_2{\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}}}_2\). This is represented by the parallelogram \(ODBE\); the segments \(OD\) and \(EB\) correspond to the vector \({\mathbf{X}}_2{\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}}}_2\), while \(DB\) and \(OE\) are shifted copies of \({\mathbf{X}}_2{\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}}}_2\).

Figure 3.3: Triangle ABC

Lastly, the triangle \(BCE\) gives yet another decomposition.

Figure 3.4: Triangle BCE

We can deduce from the latter the formula for least squares. Looking at the residuals in the second picture, we see that the segment \(AC\) is also the residual from the FWL regression.
